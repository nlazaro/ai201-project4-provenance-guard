# Provenance Guard — planning.md

A backend that classifies whether submitted text was written by a human or generated by AI,
scores its confidence honestly, surfaces a plain-language transparency label, and lets
creators appeal. This document is the spec the implementation is built against.

---

## Problem & architecture narrative

A creator submits text through `POST /submit`. The text is run through **three independent
detection signals**, whose outputs are blended into a single `ai_likelihood` (P(AI)) and a
separate `confidence` (how sure we are of the verdict). That produces an `attribution`
(`likely_ai` / `likely_human` / `uncertain`) and a **transparency label**. The submission, its
signal scores, and its label are written to a structured **audit log**, and the response is
returned to the platform. If a creator disagrees, `POST /appeal` flips the content's status to
`under_review` and logs the appeal next to the original decision for a human reviewer.

**Submission flow:** `POST /submit` → signal 1 (LLM) + signal 2 (stylometry) + signal 3
(lexical) → `combine()` (ai_likelihood + confidence) → `classify()` (attribution) →
`make_label()` → audit log → JSON response.
**Appeal flow:** `POST /appeal` → look up content → status → `under_review` → audit log (appeal
beside original decision) → confirmation response.

## 1. Detection signals

Three genuinely distinct signals (semantic, structural, lexical). Each returns a P(AI) in
`[0,1]`; higher = more AI-like.

**Signal 1 — LLM semantic (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`).**
- *Measures:* holistic semantic/stylistic coherence — does the text "read" machine-generated?
- *Why it differs:* humans break patterns, take idiosyncratic turns, and make local choices a
  model smooths over. An LLM judge captures this gestalt.
- *Blind spot:* over-trusts polished human writing; can be inconsistent on short text; costs an
  API call and can fail (handled by graceful degradation).
- *Output:* `{"score": float}` or `score=None` if the API/key is unavailable.

**Signal 2 — Stylometric structure (pure Python).**
- *Measures:* burstiness (coefficient of variation of sentence length), type-token ratio
  (vocabulary diversity), informality density (contractions / bare lowercase "i"), mean
  sentence length.
- *Why it differs:* AI text is statistically *uniform* — even sentence lengths, low
  burstiness, few contractions. Human writing is *variable* and bursty.
- *Blind spot:* conflates **formality** with AI. Formal, uniform human writing (academic
  prose, non-native-English writers) scores high. This is the system's main false-positive
  risk and is deliberately contained (see §2).
- *Output:* `{"score": float, "features": {...}}`.

**Signal 3 — Lexical AI-tells (pure Python).**
- *Measures:* density of known LLM cliché/transition phrases ("it is important to note,"
  "furthermore," "delve," "stakeholders," "paradigm shift," ...).
- *Why it differs:* current models overuse a recognizable register of connective and
  hedging phrases at higher rates than most human writing.
- *Blind spot:* trivially evaded by editing; absent in short text; some formal humans use these
  phrases. High precision when it fires, low recall.
- *Output:* `{"score": float, "hits": [...]}`.

**Combination (ensemble weighting):** `ai_likelihood = 0.60·LLM + 0.25·stylo + 0.15·lexical`
(re-normalized if the LLM signal is unavailable). The LLM is weighted highest because it is the
most reliable single judge; the cheaper structural/lexical signals correct and corroborate it.

## 2. Uncertainty representation

Two numbers are kept distinct on purpose:
- **`ai_likelihood`** = P(AI), sets *direction*.
- **`confidence`** = how sure we are of the *verdict* (not P(AI)). `confidence =
  |ai_likelihood − 0.5|·2`, then **reduced in proportion to signal disagreement**.

A confidence of **0.6** means: the signals agree and point clearly one way, but not
overwhelmingly — enough to show a directional label, not enough to present it as near-certain.

**Disagreement drives uncertainty.** The uncertain band around 0.5 is *dynamic*: its half-width
is `0.12 + 0.28·disagreement`, where `disagreement` is the spread between signal scores. When
signals conflict, the band widens and the verdict becomes `uncertain` rather than a coin-flip.

**Thresholds (with false-positive asymmetry).** Because labeling a human's work as AI is the
costliest error on a writing platform, asserting **AI** requires clearing an *extra* `+0.04`
margin beyond the human side. Concretely, with agreeing signals: `ai_likelihood ≥ ~0.66` →
`likely_ai`; `≤ ~0.38` → `likely_human`; otherwise `uncertain`. Texts under 25 words are forced
to `uncertain` (too little signal).

**How meaningfulness was tested:** four deliberately chosen inputs (clear-AI, clear-human, formal-human, edited-AI) were run through `test_signals.py`. Scores must span the range and reach all three labels; the formal-human case must *not* be called AI. (Results in README §Confidence scoring.)

## 3. Transparency label — three variants

Written for a non-technical reader; `{pct}` is the estimated likelihood as a percentage. The AI variant
is deliberately hedged and always offers the appeal path (false-positive protection).

- **High-confidence AI:** "🤖 Likely AI-generated. Our automated check estimates roughly a
  {pct}% likelihood that this text was produced with substantial help from an AI writing tool. This is
  an automated estimate, not a certainty — if you wrote this yourself, you can appeal and a
  human will review it."
- **High-confidence human:** "✍️ Likely human-written. Our automated check estimates roughly a
  {pct}% likelihood that a person wrote this text; no strong AI-generation signals stood out. Automated
  estimates can still be wrong, and any creator may appeal a result."
- **Uncertain:** "❓ Uncertain. Our automated check could not reach a confident conclusion about
  whether this text was written by a person or an AI tool — the signals were mixed or too weak
  to call. We are showing this honestly rather than guessing; no attribution is being asserted."

## 4. Appeals workflow

- *Who:* any creator, using the `content_id` from their `/submit` response.
- *What they provide:* `creator_reasoning` (free text — why they believe the call is wrong).
- *What the system does:* validates the `content_id` (404 if unknown); updates
  `content.status` → `under_review`; writes an `appeal` entry to the audit log carrying the
  `creator_reasoning` **alongside the original attribution/confidence**; returns a confirmation
  echoing the original decision. No automated re-classification.
- *What a reviewer sees:* `GET /log` shows the appeal entry paired with the original submission
  entry for the same `content_id` — the reviewer reads the original verdict, the signal scores,
  and the creator's reasoning side by side.

## 5. Anticipated edge cases

1. **Formal / non-native-English human writing.** Uniform sentence structure and formal
   vocabulary spike the stylometric signal (its blind spot). *Containment:* the LLM signal is
   weighted higher, signal disagreement widens the uncertain band, and the AI side needs an
   extra margin — so this lands `uncertain`, not a false `likely_ai`. (Verified on the econ
   paragraph.)
2. **Very short text (a haiku, a two-line poem).** Too few sentences for burstiness/TTR to be
   meaningful and too little for the LLM to judge. *Containment:* texts under 25 words are
   forced to `uncertain` with capped confidence.
3. **Lightly edited AI / human-AI hybrids.** Genuinely ambiguous; signals straddle the middle.
   *Containment:* correctly resolves to `uncertain` rather than a false binary — which is the
   honest answer, even if a platform wanting a hard call would see it as a miss.

## AI Tool Plan

**M3 — submission endpoint + first signal.** Provide: §1 Detection signals + the Architecture
diagram. Ask the AI to generate the Flask skeleton with the `POST /submit` stub and the LLM
signal function. Verify: call `llm_signal()` directly on 2–3 inputs and confirm it returns a
`{"score": float}` in range and matches the contract before wiring it into the route.

**M4 — second/third signal + confidence scoring.** Provide: §1 + §2 Uncertainty + diagram. Ask
for the stylometric and lexical functions and the `combine()` scoring logic. Verify against the
spec thresholds: run `test_signals.py` on all four canonical inputs and confirm scores vary and
reach three labels, and that the formal-human case is **not** called AI. Correct the weights if
the generated scoring diverges from the specified ranges.

**M5 — production layer.** Provide: §3 Label variants + §4 Appeals + diagram. Ask for
`make_label()` (score → label text) and the `POST /appeal` endpoint. Verify: confirm all three
label variants are reachable by submitting inputs at different confidence levels, and that an
appeal flips status to `under_review` and logs the reasoning beside the original decision.
